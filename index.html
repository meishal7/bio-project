Life definition

Life definition

Biologists define 5 basic qualities of life

  • All living things are composed of one, or more, recognizable units called the cell.
  • All living things carry out metabolism, the manipulation of energy.
  • All living things have the ability to detect and respond to their environment.
  • All living things have the capability to grow, develop, and reproduce, processes that are directed from within by DNA.
  • All living things, as a population of organisms, have the ability to evolve over the course of many generations.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates

— is a collection of organic molecules is characterized as being composed of C, H, and O in a specific ratio of C1H2O1. The smaller, simpler representatives of this category are called simple sugars

  • FAST source of energy
  • Polar, readily dissolving in water and body fluids
  • Monomers of carbohydrates are monosaccharides e.g., glucose and fructose
  • Two monosaccharides connected together makes a disaccharide. For example, in sucrose (table sugar), a glucose and fructose link together.

Carbohydrates

The four primary functions of carbohydrates in the body are

  • Provide energy
  • Store energy
  • Build macromolecules
  • Spare protein and fat for other uses

Proteins

Proteins

— is a naturally occurring, extremely complex substance that consists of amino acid residues joined by peptide bonds. Proteins are present in all living organisms and include many essential biological compounds such as enzymes, hormones, and antibodies.

  • Hydrophilic backbone
  • Chain folds to assume most comfortable or energetically stable shape
  • Protein function depends on Amino Acid shape

Antibodies

— also called immunoglobulins, a protective proteins produced by the immune system in response to the presence of a foreign substance, called an antigen. Antibodies recognize and latch onto antigens in order to remove them from the body. A wide range of substances are regarded by the body as antigens, including disease-causing organisms and toxic materials such as insect venom.

Receptors

— Receptors are generally transmembrane proteins, which bind to signaling molecules outside the cell and subsequently transmit the signal through a sequence of molecular switches to internal signaling pathways.

Functions

  • Binds to specific signaling molecules outside membrane
  • Triggers change in structure of protein molecules

KERATINS

— fibrous structural proteins of hair, nails, horn, hoofs, wool, feathers, and of the epithelial cells in the outermost layers of the skin. Keratin serves important structural and protective functions, particularly in the epithelium. Some keratins have also been found to regulate key cellular activities, such as cell growth and protein synthesis.

NUCLEIC ACID

NUCLEIC ACID

— naturally occurring chemical compound that is capable of being broken down to yield phosphoric acid, sugars, and a mixture of organic bases (purines and pyrimidines). Nucleic acids are the main information-carrying molecules of the cell, and, by directing the process of protein synthesis, they determine the inherited characteristics of every living thing. The two main classes of nucleic acids are deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and ribonucleic acid (RNA). DNA is the master blueprint for life and constitutes the genetic material in all free-living organisms and most viruses. RNA is the genetic material of certain viruses, but it is also found in all living cells, where it plays an important role in certain processes such as the making of proteins.

  • Monomer: Nucleotide
  • Name of nucleotide varies depending on base and present phosphate group
  • Key to transmission of genetic information lies in 4 bases
    • Adenine (A)
    • Guanine (G)
    • Cytosine (C)
    • Thymine (T)
  • Nucleotide held together by bonds between sugar and phosphate entities– Sugar phosphate backbone [(within DNA)
  • Backbone is hydrophilic while complementary base pairs are hydrophobic

DNA

— is a polymer of the four nucleotides A, C, G, and T, which are joined through a backbone of alternating phosphate and deoxyribose sugar residues. These nitrogen-containing bases occur in complementary pairs as determined by their ability to form hydrogen bonds between them. A always pairs with T through two hydrogen bonds, and G always pairs with C through three hydrogen bonds. The spans of A:T and G:C hydrogen-bonded pairs are nearly identical, allowing them to bridge the sugar-phosphate chains uniformly. This structure, along with the moleculeâ€™s chemical stability, makes DNA the ideal genetic material. The bonding between complementary bases also provides a mechanism for the replication of DNA and the transmission of genetic information.

RNA

— RNA is a single-stranded nucleic acid polymer of the four nucleotides A, C, G, and U joined through a backbone of alternating phosphate and ribose sugar residues. It is the first intermediate in converting the information from DNA into proteins essential for the working of a cell. Some RNAs also serve direct roles in cellular metabolism. RNA is made by copying the base sequence of a section of double-stranded DNA, called a gene, into a piece of single-stranded nucleic acid. This process, called transcription (see below RNA metabolism), is catalyzed by an enzyme called RNA polymerase.
